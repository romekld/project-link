Chapter 1 
Introduction 
 
 
A. Background 
 
The Field Health Services Information System (FHSIS)  is a nationwide facility -based 
recording and reporting system that provides information to the Local Government 
Units (LGUs) and the Department of Health (DOH) as basis for decision -making 
relative to the management and implementation of public health programs throughout 
the country. As stipulated in Executive Order No. 352 – Annex 1, the FHSIS is one of 
the statistical activities designated by the then National Statistical Coordination Board 
(NSCB) that will generate critical data for decision-making by administrators, planners 
and policy makers in the government and private sector. The following summarizes 
the evolution of the FHSIS as a health management information system. 
 
1987. The FHSIS was conceptualized in response to the need to streamline the initial 
DOH reporting system which was found by the midwives to be burdensome and time-
consuming, and which prevented them from performing their primary task of 
delivering services.   
 
1989. It took 2 years to design the FHSIS with the pertinent recording and reporting 
tools/forms to be used as well as the training of all concerned health staff at various 
levels of operations. After a series of consultations with all the national program 
coordinators together with their regional and local counterparts, the FHSIS was 
launched and implemented nationwide . This tasked all health facilities nationwide 
from the Barangay Health Stations (BHSs) and Rural Health Units (RHUs) to record 
data, generate and submit reports to the next level of administration until these reach 
the national level. 
  
1991. Ba rely a year after the full implementation of FHSIS, the Local Government 
Code (LGC) was implemente d decentralizing the delivery of health services to the 
LGUs. To make the FHSIS relevant and responsive to the changes brought about by 
the LGC, some modifications were introduced: (i) the simplification and shortening of 
the Summary Tables (ST); and (ii) reducing the over-dependence on computers in the 
production of these STs. These changes constituted the Modified FHSIS (MFHSIS). 
 
1996. The MFHSIS was  implemented nationwide beginning at this year. However, 
despite the innovations, the system continued to experience problems in its 
operations including poor utilization of data for decision making by leaders at various 
levels of the health system, and the sub -optimal quality of the data characterized by 
delayed submissions and incomplete reports. 
 
2001. Five years later, a nother revision  was undertaken which resulted to the 
Decentralized FHSIS (DFHSIS). The DFHSIS was piloted in six areas nationwide 
(three provinces and three cities) in an effort to ad dress the shortcomings of the

B.  Rationale for 2018 Version of the FHSIS 
 
After 5 years of implementing the FHSIS version 2012, the DOH-EB considered it 
timely to update the FHSIS Manual of Operations (MOP) in view of the following 
developments:  
 
(1) The DOH through the National Monitoring and Evaluation/Data Governance 
Committee has called for the harmonization of all essential health indicators 
currently collected through the different DOH data/information health systems. 
This requires that indicators being measured or tracked t hrough the FHSIS must 
bear the same definition and measurements as those collected or reported 
through other health information systems. 
 
(2) The administration’s (previous and current) focus of serving the poor necessitates 
the disaggregation of service cover age among the poor (e.g. NHTS HHs given 
services). 
 
(3) The goal to establish a functional and responsive service delivery network in the 
health sector recognizes and further supports the need to record and report 
service coverage not only those provided by th e BHSs and RHUs but also by 
other providers of similar services such as the Out -Patient Department (OPD) of

C. Purpose of the FHSIS MOP 
 
The 2018  FHSIS MOP serves as the overall reference in the establishment and 
operationalization of the FHSIS at various levels of operations. As a reference, it 
guides local health managers and staff to collect and generate information useful to 
the LGUs in improving access to quality services and in managing more efficiently 
and effectively the various public health programs in their respective localities  
Moreover, the MOP also provides the DOH at the regional and national level with a  
clearly established set of public healt h program indicators to be tracked and 
monitored nationwide, the results of  which are expected to guide policy formulation, 
resource allocation and prioritization, and other essential decision-making processes. 
Specifically, the 2018 FHSIS MOP aims to: 
 
(1) summarize, rationalize and define the indicators to be collected and 
reported through the FHSIS in line with the DOH overall health sector 
monitoring and evaluation framework;

D. Methodology  
 
The development of the 20 18 FHSIS MOP was spearheaded by the DOH - 
Epidemiology Bureau (EB) with support from the Technical Working Group (TWG) 
specifically established for this purpose. The TWG is composed of representatives 
from the different health programs  and concerned DOH offices/units involved in 
monitoring program performance  including representative staff from the Phil ippine 
Statistics Authority (PSA). Series of consultations were undertaken to assess the 
current version of the FHSIS, review and critique the metadata to be collected and 
evaluate newly proposed indicators to be covered. These consultations were done 
through organized group workshops and individual meetings with the concerned DOH 
offices.  
 
Results of the FHSIS workshops and trainings held in 2016 provided initial inputs on 
the areas needing enhancement of the current version of the FHSIS. The Data 
Reconciliation Workshop spearheaded by the DOH -Health Policy and Plan 
Development Bureau (HPDPB) in May 2018 further concretized the actions and 
decisions on key issues raised . Recommendations by the Technical Working Group 
(TWG) on M and E/Data Governance Committee further guided the revision and 
enhancement of the 2012 FHSIS. 
 
The enhanced version of the 2018 FHSIS MOP once completed was presented to a 
group of national and regional program coordinators, FHSIS coordinators including 
local implementers (nurse, m idwives, sanitary inspectors, etc.) for further review and 
validation. Follow-up meetings with concerned DOH offices with unresolved program 
policy and technical -related issues were conducted. All these inputs and comments  
were incorporated into the MOP  which was later pre -tested in selected facilities in 
Laguna and Mar ikina City.  Pre-Test participants were given orientation on the 
mechanics of the pre-test and to prepare them for the actual activity.   
  
The final version of this 2018 FHSIS MOP was based on the results of the pre -tests 
and further inputs from the TWG members. Please refer to Annex 1 for the list of 
people consulted in the enhancement of the MOP.

E. Scope and Limitation of the FHSIS MOP 
 
Note that the 2018 FHSIS does not monitor and track the accomplishment of all public 
health programs and neither does it cover all the indicators identified by the Program 
Coordinators. Beyond these program accomplishments though are another set of 
demographic, morbidity and mortality data that are tracked on an annual basis. 
 
E.1 Program Accomplishment/Coverage of Public Health Services 
 
1. Family Health Services:  Family Planning (FP), Maternal Care, Child Care, 
Management of Sick Children, Oral Health. Micronutrient Supplementation 
and deworming coverage are integrated into the maternal care and child 
care. Special indicators on Adolescent Health service coverage are also 
accounted for through the age -disaggregation by  10-14 and 15 -19 age 
groups under the Family Planning, Maternal Care and Oral Health Services. 
 
2. Infectious Disease Prevention and Control Services : The Filariasis, 
Schistosomiasis, S oil-Transmitted Helminthiasis (S TH) and S exually 
Transmitted Infection (S TI) Prevention and Contro l Programs are totally 
dependent on the FHSIS in tracking their respective accomplishments. 
Hence, FHSIS covered most ly the essential indicators identified by these 
programs. On the other hand, several Infectious Diseas e Prevention and 
Control Programs have established their own management information 
systems which ar e fully functional. Hence, the FHSIS only covered a 
minimum set of  indicators to report and the data being sourced from their 
respective program management information systems. These include: (i) 
National Tuberculosis Program (N TP): Integrated Tuberculosis Information 
System (ITIS); (ii) Malaria Control Program: Philippine Malaria Management 
Information System (PhilMIS) ; (iii) Leprosy Prevention and Control : 
Integrated Leprosy Information System (ILIS) ; and (iv) Rabies Prevention 
and Control Program: National Rabies Information System (NaRIS).  
 
3. Non-Communicable Diseases.   For the first time, service coverage of 
selected lifestyle–related diseases (LRDs) are incorporated into the FHSIS. 
These include coverage of risk assessment using the Philippine Protocol on 
Essential Non-Communicable diseases (PhilPEN) tool, th ose identified with 
risks (smoking, alcohol use, physical inactivity and unhealthy diet) including  
those risk assessed for breast mass and cervical cancer, d iabetes mellitus 
and hypertension and those with visual acuity problems.   
 
4. Environmental Health and Sanitation. The 2018 FHSIS MOP continues to 
collect and report on safe water and sanitation services including the 
practice of solid waste management as well as issuances of health 
certificates to food and industrial institutions. The proportion of barangays 
declared zero-open defecation (ZOD) area is now included in the FHSIS.

E.2 Morbidity and Mortality Data 
 
The 2018 FHSIS MOP will continue to collect and report morbidity and mor tality data 
on an annual basis resulting from v arying causes, and to be expressed as incidence 
rates over total population X 100,000 populat ion. Likewise, top ten leading causes of 
morbidity and mortality (for all ages) will also be computed and reported. Special 
mortality rates shall also be computed and reported: 
 
1. Maternal Mortality Ratio per 100,000 population 
2. Under-five Mortality Rate per 1,000 live births 
3. Infant Mortality Rate per 1,000 live births 
4. Neonatal Mortality Rate per 1,000 live births: new indicator 
 
E.3 Demographic Data  
 
The 201 8 FHSIS MOP will continue to provide information on annual basis the 
following demographic and natality information. Most of the previous natality-related 
data have been placed under the Maternal Care  (e.g. facility-based deliveries, b irths 
attended by skilled health professionals, outcome of pregnancy and categorization of 
birth weight). 
 
 Total population 
 Total number of households 
 Total number of live births 
 Total number of public local health workers: doctors, nurses, midwives, 
medical technologist s, nutritionists, sanitary engineers, sanitary 
inspectors 
 Total number of Active Barangay Health Workers 
 Total number of Barangay Health Stations 
 Total number of Main Health Centers and Rural Health Units 
 
E.4 Limitations 
 
Note that the 201 8 FHSIS Version does not cover the accomplishments or service 
coverage of all public health programs. It does not also ge nerate data on health 
governance, financing and regulations as these are sourced from other offices/units 
outside the health facility. Specific limitations include: 
 
 does not cover all the public health programs of the DOH  especially the 
new developing/em erging programs which have not yet fully and 
concretely developed their meta data. (e.g. mental health program, drug 
rehabilitation program, etc.) 
 
 only reports on selected service coverage indicators depending on the 
maturity of the programs  are collected through FHSIS (e.g. other LRDs 
and essential NCDs)

F. How to Use the MOP 
 
There are 9 chapters of the MOP including this Introduction  Chapter. It is advisable 
that every manager and implementer of the FHSIS must refer to this document and 
must read the first 2 chapters to have an overall view of the content and structure of 
the whole MOP.  
 
Chapter 1.   Introduction 
Describes the overall purpose of the  FHSIS MOP, its scope and 
limitations including the outline of the different chapters 
 
Chapter 2.   2018 Field Health Service Information System 
Describes the objectives, process and the key elements of the FHSIS 
as a management information system. At the last part of the Chapter is 
the summary of roles and responsibilities of the different entities 
engaged in the implementation and management of the FHSIS at all 
levels of operations 
 
Chapter 3.  Profiling of Households 
This Chapter recommends the profiling of households as the first basic 
step in identifying and mapping all HHs to be served by the health 
facility in a given area.
