Report Dissemination and Utilization 
 
 
A. Report Dissemination and Utilization  
 
The final step in any monitoring activity through the FHSIS is the translation of data 
into relevant information as basis for decision ‐making. This requires packaging, 
communication and dissemination of data into a format and language understandable 
to the  identified users of the information. These may include program coordinators, 
policy makers, service providers and other relevant groups of stakeholders.  Results 
of the FHSIS can be used at various levels of the health system specifically for health 
service management, health system management, planning, advocacy and policy 
development, resource prioritization and allocation.  
 
B. Basic Principles in Report Dissemination 
 
Dissemination of program accomplishments is a critical step in the overall monitoring 
system as it determines:  (i) what is to be reported, (ii) when to be reported and (iii) to 
whom it should be reported. The dissemination of reports is guided by the following 
principles: 
 
1. Dissemination should be planned for the unique characteristics of eac h 
targeted user or audience.  
 
2. The most effective packaging and channels of communication in 
disseminating the results must be selected.  
 
3. The timing of information dissemination should be planned carefully to fit in 
with the planning cycles and needs of users.  
 
4. Monitoring results should be continuously disseminated to provide feedback to 
decision-makers.  
 
5. The most effective mechanism to develop and institute an information culture 
is to link the data/information obtained to actual resource allocation (budgets) 
and developing indicator‐driven plan.  
 
C. Uses of Reports 
 
Monitoring reports can play many different roles, and the information produced can be 
put to very different uses such as to: 
 
1. demonstrate accountability: delivering on political promises  made to citizenry 
and other stakeholders

D. Guide in Dissemination 
 
1. Know the audience/s and target  
 
1.1 Know the audience/s and how they want to see the information expressed. 
 
1.2  Take into account their interests, expectations and preferred 
communication medium.  
 
2. Develop a communications strategy that will address the following questions. 
It should take into account the challenges in communicating results to different 
stakeholders.  
 
• Who will receive what information?  
• In what format?  
• When? 
 • Who will prepare the information?  
• Who will deliver the information?  
 
3. Ensure that everyone concerned is informed of the progress and findings even 
during the on-going process of monitoring and evaluation. Continue to provide 
feedback.  
 
3.1 Use Informal means: phone, e-mail, fax, personal conversations, etc. 
 
3.2 Use formal communication means: briefings, presentations, written reports 
 
4. Present data in a short and crisp manner and be relevant to the target 
audience.

4.1 Present only the most important data.  The report can be well targeted with 
clear definition of its audience/s and the types of questions that audience 
is likely to raise about findings.  
 
4.2 Large “data dumps” of information are counterproductive. Know what the 
decision makers want and provide them with the necessary information in 
the format with which they are most comfortable. This may require tailoring 
information in the preferred format f or each of the decision makers/end 
users.  
 
4.3 If there are multiple audiences (e.g. those involved at the project, program, 
and policy levels), the data may have to be packaged and formatted 
differently according to the main interests and preferences of each  
audience.  
 
5. Clear the report with all key parties before it is formally presented. This will 
help to eliminate errors and will also ensure that many points are clarified 
informally without the embarrassment of confrontations 
 
6. Anticipate further disseminat ion of performance findings to a broader 
audience. Typically, the higher up the chain of command, the less need there 
is for extensive detail and explanation; aggregated, succinct data relevant to 
the specific issue will be more appropriate.  
 
6.1 Personal briefings, especially to high -level officials can be another 
effective means of communicating performance findings.  
 
6.2 Further down the managerial chain, it is more likely that more operational 
data will be desired.  
 
7. Report performance data in comparison to earlier data and to the baseline.  
 
8. Include actions required as decision makers may be looking for some 
indications in response to data findings. They will also be interested in 
available options (including costs, pros and cons, and the like) wi th respect to 
acting on performance findings throughout the process.  
 
9. Highlight the implications of recommended actions.  “Simply recommending 
that certain actions be taken is rarely sufficient. Officials will usually want a 
fuller understanding of the im plications of their action. Wise evaluators 
anticipate this need and provide, whenever possible, best estimates (or 
perhaps a range of estimates) of both the costs and consequences of the 
recommendations”  
 
10.  For follow-up and feedback, set up a process to bring together stakeholders 
and evaluators to discuss findings, insights, alternative actions and next steps.

E. Presentation of Data / Report 
 
1. Comparative Reporting 
 
1.1 Comparing actual performance to program targets is central to reporting 
results. Always report against the desired performance level to determine 
whether efforts undertaken have resulted to the desired outcome or 
impact. 
 
1.2 Comparison of data over time are critical. Report against the baseline and 
intermediate measurements to determine w hether progress has been 
sustained, whether there was only a short spurt of improvement, or 
whether early improvements have all disappeared. 
 
1.3 Comparison by geographic areas or administrative subdivisions is useful in 
identifying which areas performed well and which ones are pulling down 
the national/regional/provincial/city coverage.  
 
2. Presentation of Data  
 
2.1 Present data in a simple, clear and easily understandable format.  
 
2.2 Present only the most important data.  
 
2.3 Avoid acronyms and jargon. 
 
2.4 Provide a minimum of background information to establish the context. 
 
2.5 State major points up front. 
 
2.6 Organize findings and recommendations around key outcomes and their 
indicators.  
 
2.7 Use a separate appendix or report to convey detailed data.

3. Four  Dimensions of Reporting  
 
3.1 Written Summaries 
 
 To be a useful management tool, the written summary should contain and 
present the following: 
 
a. An introduction including purpose of report, monitoring questions, program 
background, and program goals and objectives. 
 
b. A description of the monitoring methodology including focus, evaluation 
focus, limitations of methodology, who performed the 
monitoring/evaluation, and when the monitoring/ evaluation was 
performed).  
c.  Present data on findings selectively and in an understandable manner; 
organize data around study questions, major themes or program 
components; and use charts and tables.  
 
d. Conclusions should be clearly connected to evidence on performance. 
Evidence should be presented to support recommendations.  
 
3.2 Executive Summaries 
 
a.  Executive summaries should be short: preferably one to four pages.  
 
b. Present major findings and recommendations in bullet format. 
 
c. Refer the readers/users to the main text of the report or appendices for 
more details.  
 
d. It should contain a brief overview, including the background and purpose 
of the study.  
 
e. It should also include a brief description of major questions, issues, and 
research methods.  
 
3.3 Oral Presentations 
 
Oral presentations can also be used, either alone or in conjunction with a 
written report.  
 
a. Consider the following in preparing for an oral presentation: 
o Who is the audience?  
o What should they remember from the presentation?  
o How much time is there for the presentation?  
o What are the available delivery resources?  
o What handouts should be provided, if any?

b. Oral presentations should be simple, clear, and tailored to the audience.  
 
c. Avoid complex language and detailed data 
 
d. Organize the presentation well. If possible, use an interactive format with 
the audience, and be prepared for questions.  
 
e. Rehearse the presentation and also how to get feedback. 
 
3.4 Visual Presentations 
 
Visual presentations e.g. charts, graphs, and maps are helpful in highlighting 
key points and performance findings. They can illustrate directions and trends 
at a glance. Please refer to Chapter 9.2. Data Analysis and Interpretation for 
the different types of visualization for the presentation. 
 
a. The purpose of using visualization is to describe, explore, tabulate, and 
compare. These can provide impact and visual interest, encourage 
audience acceptance and memory retention, and show the big picture. 
 
b. Visual presentations of evidence should be governed by principles of 
reasoning about quantitative evidence. Clear and precise seeing becomes 
as one with clear and precise thinking. 
 
c. There are many different reporting formats including written reports and 
displays. It is important to check with users and stakeholders for any 
preferences for data presentation. Be cautious not to use inappropriate 
visuals just because they may be popular.  
 
4. Presenting/Disseminating Poor Results 
 
One cannot manage by receiving only good news. A good performance 
measurement system is intended to surface problems, not just bring good 
news. Reporting on bad news is a critical aspect of how one distinguishes 
success from failure. If the difference cann ot be determined, it is likely that 
both failure and success are being rewarded by managers. A good 
performance system can serve as a kind of early warning system.  
 
4.1 Include in the performance reports explanations (if possible) about poor 
outcomes and identify steps taken or planned to correct problems. 
 
4.2 Messengers should not be punished for delivering bad news. Instilling fear 
of bringing forth bad news will not encourage reporting and use of findings. 
 
4.3 The value of information often decreases rapidly over  time, so essential 
findings should be communicated as quickly as possible.”

F. Data Utilization 
 
After examining effective ways of reporting, it is important to highlight important 
factors to consider in maximizing the use of the information presented o r 
disseminated:   (1) uses of performance findings; (2) additional benefits of using the 
findings: feedback, knowledge, and learning; and (3) strategies for sharing 
information.  
 
1. Uses of Performance Findings. 
 
1.1 Using findings to improve performance is the main purpose of the FHSIS.  Its 
main purpose is not simply to generate continuous results -based information, 
but to get that information to the appropriate users in a timely fashion so that 
the performance feedback can be used to better manage programs.  
 
1.2  Using the FHSIS performance information will  inform the concerned DOH 
offices on the following areas of concern:    
 
a. inform DOH management decisions that can lead to increase or decrease 
in budget allocation of programs/unit; 
 
b. enhance or expand DOH policies, programs and projects or may also stop 
or eliminate current policies and standards;   
 
c. allow DOH management options to offer incentives (monetary and 
nonmonetary) to office/unit/personnel for good performance; 
 
d. appreciate their contribution to the achievement of overall goal and 
objectives.  
 
1.3 Results of FHSIS can also be maximized for the following purposes:  
 
o Respond to elected officials’ and public’s demands for accountability 
 
o Help formulate and justify budget requests  
 
o Help make operational resource allocation decisions  
 
o Trigger in-depth examinations of what performance problems exist and 
what corrections are needed  
 
o Help motivate personnel to continue making program improvements  
 
o Formulate and monitor the performance of contractors and grantees 
 
o Provide data for special, in-depth program evaluations

2. Additional Benefits of Using Findings. 
 
2.1 Knowledge, and Learning.   FHSIS findings can promote knowledge and 
learning in the DOH and its allied agencies. The new emphasis in the 
international aid community is more and more on local knowledge acquisition, 
not knowledge transfer from donor to recipient.  
 
a. Knowledge and know ledge management are additional components of 
using results of the performance monitoring. New knowledge can be 
generated through the use of findings on a continuous basis. Knowledge 
management means capturing findings, institutionalizing learning, and 
organizing the wealth of information produced continually.  
 
b. Results of FHSIS have a special capacity to add to the learning and 
knowledge process. When used effectively, it can be an institutionalized 
form of learning and knowledge. “Learning must therefore be incorporated 
into the overall programming cycle. Information must be disseminated and 
available to potential users in order to become applied knowledge.  
 
c. Institutionalizing learning is important in governments and organizations. 
Policy and program evaluation play a systematic instead of an ad hoc role 
in the process of organizational learning. Emphasizing organizational 
learning as a means of enhancing organizational performance is a fruitful 
and promising area of engagement with the public sector.  
 
2.2 Feedback. The FHSIS can provide important feedback about the progress, as 
well as the success or failure of DOH policies, programs and projects. This 
constitutes a powerful, continuous public management tool that DOH 
management can use to improve performance , and demonstrate 
accountability and transparency with respect to results. By producing a 
continual flow of feedback, the FHSIS can: 
 
a. help DOH management and middle program managers coordinate more 
effectively policies, programs and interventions.
