Chapter 9.2 
Data Analysis and Interpretation 
 
 
 
A.  Overall Process in Data Analysis and Interpretation  
 
The overall purpose of the FHSIS is to generate data to be used by the national, 
regional and local program managers and other stakeholders in enhancing the 
implementation of their different public health programs. The utilization of these data 
can only be made possible if the FHSIS data are properly analyzed and interpreted. 
Data management plays an important role in improving the performance of the healt h 
sector. Collecting, analyzing, interpreting, and acting on data for specific performance 
measures allows health policy makers and program managers to identify where 
programs or systems are falling short, to make corrective adjustments strategies, and 
to track outcomes.  Basically, there are basically 4 steps in analyzing and interpreting 
data:

and

C.  Ready the Data for Analysis (Step 2) 
 
1. Review the accomplished Data Collection Forms presented in the previous

2. Double check each entry for completeness and accuracy. 
 
3. It is best to enter the data by computer to facilitate your consolidation and 
analysis. Most of the data collection forms for the Health Service Provider

4. Re-encode data as needed depending on the analysis technique to be used 
 
D.  Analyze and Interpret the Data (Step 3) 
Data analysis and interpretation is the process of assigning meaning to the 
data/information collected and determining the significance and  implications of the 
findings.   
 
1. Presentation of Data 
Present data in appropriate formats so that these can easily be analyzed and 
understood. Examples are charts, tables and graphs. 
 
2. Analyze and Interpret the Data 
There are different approaches in interpreting the data you have collected and 
processed.  
 
2.1 Compare the data with the goal or target 
Simply establish if the desired performance level set for each of the indicators 
was met or not. Desired levels are usually  reflected in the program goals or

2.2 Analyze data over time 
 
It is also valuable to analyze the data over time to allow you to track the 
progress of performance.  This is most useful if you have established baseline 
levels prior to the introduction of programs or interventions. You have to ask 
the following questions: 
 
 Is the trend of performance over time improving? What could have been 
the factors that contributed to improved performance level?  
 Is there a decreasing trend of performance over the time periods? What 
are the factors that worsen the performance? 
 Are the results fluctuating? What factors influence performance per period 
being compared? Are there measurement issues related to the fluctuating 
trend (e.g. a change in definition of the indicator, outbreaks in between, 
stock outs of drugs or essential commodities during specific years).

2.3 Analyze data by geographical location 
 
It is worth analyzing data by geographic distribution especially on program 
performance. This will allow you to identify areas which are not performing 
well, and therefore may need further assistance. In this analysis, you may 
need to identify areas which have reached the targeted performance levels 
and which ones are below par. You may cite the range of performance levels 
or do the ranking of areas (e.g. from the area with highest performance to the 
area with lowest level. You may apply this to data with r egional breakdown, or 
even at the provincial/city/municipal level.  
 
a. You may ask the following questions when analyzing data by area: 
 
 Which area/s has achieved the desired performance level? What are the 
possible common characteristics that explain their successful 
achievements? 
 
 Which area/s are pulling down the national average. What could be the 
reasons for inability to reach the desired targets? 
 
 How many area/s of the total areas reached the targeted performance 
level? Are there more areas that achiev e the target or there are more 
who did not? Could there be factors related to the program/intervention 
that were not properly implemented?

2.4 Correlate results with other findings on related indicators 
  
 Monitoring health sector performance requires a deeper analysis of 
indicators. It is necessary that correlations be made among related and 
supported indicators to fully assess achievements in the health sector. The 
following are examples that need to be analyzed and correlated:  
 
a. resource investments vis-à-vis level of  health outcome or impact  
b. PhilHealth coverage, enrollment  vis-à-vis out-of-pocket expenses 
c. health human resource ratio to population vis-à-vis health service 
coverage or outcome 
d. policy compliance to service coverage levels 
e. service access vis-à-vis quality of health care 
 
3. Reflect on the Results (STEP 4) 
 
Now that data have been collected and analyzed, compare the measures to the 
desired improvement goals. If the results meet or exceed your goals, it is still 
important to continue measuring at regular intervals to monitor quality. If the 
measures show room for improvement, you may find it necessary to launch a quality 
improvement project. 
 
3.1 To implement responsive measures, it is necessary to examine the underlying 
causes behind the data you have collected. What possible factors may be 
contributing or limiting the achievement of the targets? 
 
3.2 Discuss how to address the identified pro blems or bottlenecks.  Solutions could 
vary from one performance indicator to another. 
 
3.3 The following questions can guide you in reflecting on the results: 
 
 What lessons have we learned? 
 What conclusions can be drawn from the analysis 
 What are the recommendations to improve program performance 
 
3.4 Observe problems arising during data collection and analysis. Some of these are 
technical issues resulting from underlying problems with the data collection method 
or the data itself. In this case, you need to correct data by making adjustments to 
your data collection plan. Some potential adjustments to consider include: 
 
 Are more data needed for complete analysis?  
 Is the data collected sufficient to construct quality measures and answer 
your monitoring questions?   
 Do you need to collect data on other aspects of care? 
 Are there underlying problems with the data?  Is the data collected 
consistent and thorough?
